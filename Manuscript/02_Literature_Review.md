## Literature Review

### 2.1 The Rise of AI-Generated Content

The capacity to generate realistic synthetic media has expanded rapidly since the mid-2010s,
driven by advances in generative adversarial networks, large language models, and diffusion
models (Brundage et al., 2018). Tools such as Midjourney, DALL-E, ElevenLabs, and ChatGPT
have made high-quality content generation accessible to non-technical users at negligible
cost, accelerating the volume and diversity of AI-generated content (AIGC) circulating online.
Text, images, and audio that were once distinguishable by visible artifacts or stylistic
inconsistencies have become progressively harder to identify as synthetic (Frank et al., 2024).

This development has attracted regulatory attention. The European Union's AI Act (2024)
introduces mandatory disclosure requirements for AI-generated content, and China's Provisions
on the Management of Deep Synthesis Internet Information Services (2022) similarly require
labeling of synthetic media. UNESCO (2023) has called for integrating AI literacy into
educational frameworks. These policy responses reflect a shared concern: that the public may
be insufficiently equipped to navigate an information environment increasingly populated by
synthetic content.

### 2.2 Human Ability to Detect Synthetic Media

A growing body of empirical research examines how well people can identify AI-generated
content, and the findings are consistently sobering. Nightingale and Farid (2022) demonstrated
that AI-synthesized faces were not only indistinguishable from real faces but were rated as
more trustworthy, suggesting that synthetic content may carry an unearned credibility
advantage. This result has since been replicated and extended across media types.

Frank et al. (2024) conducted the most comprehensive cross-national study to date, surveying
3,002 participants across the United States, Germany, and China. Participants were exposed to
AI-generated and human-created content across three modalities — text, image, and audio —
and asked to classify each item. The results showed that the majority of participants
effectively guessed, with detection accuracy across all media types and countries clustering
near chance level. Critically, factors typically assumed to confer an advantage — including
age, education, media literacy, and political awareness — showed no significant effect on
detection performance.

In the domain of images specifically, Roca et al. (2025) analyzed approximately 287,000
evaluations from over 12,500 participants using data from an online detection game. Overall
accuracy was 62%, only modestly above chance. Performance varied by content type: participants
were most accurate when identifying AI-generated human portraits, and least accurate with
natural and urban landscapes, suggesting that familiarity with human faces provides some
detection advantage that does not generalize to other visual content.

Text-based detection has also proven difficult. A study by Köbis et al. (2025) asked 63
university lecturers to identify AI-generated excerpts from student theses. Human evaluators
correctly identified AI-generated texts only 57% of the time — barely above chance — and their
accuracy was unrelated to teaching experience or subject-matter expertise. Together, these
findings suggest that unaided human detection of AI-generated content is unreliable across
modalities, and that neither expertise nor awareness provides meaningful protection.

### 2.3 Synthetic Media, Misinformation, and Media Trust

The societal consequences of undetectable synthetic media extend beyond individual
misjudgments. Lazer et al. (2018) documented that false news spreads faster and more broadly
than true news on social media, and Vosoughi et al. (2018) showed this disparity is driven
largely by human sharing behavior rather than automated amplification. The introduction of
AI-generated content into this environment amplifies these dynamics by lowering the production
cost of misinformation and enabling personalized fabrication at scale.

Vaccari and Chadwick (2020) examined the specific impact of deepfake political videos on
viewers and found that even when participants were uncertain whether a video was real, exposure
increased uncertainty and reduced confidence in the authenticity of other media. This
uncertainty effect — where synthetic content damages trust in genuine content — represents
a compounding social harm beyond the direct spread of false information. Pennycook and Rand
(2021) similarly identified that repeated exposure to low-credibility content degrades the
cognitive signals people use to evaluate information, making accurate judgment harder over time.

For media trust specifically, Metzger and Flanagin (2013) argued that online credibility
evaluation relies heavily on cognitive heuristics rather than systematic analysis, making users
vulnerable to surface-level cues such as visual quality and stylistic fluency — precisely the
dimensions on which modern AI-generated content performs well. As synthetic media becomes
more fluent and polished, these heuristics become less reliable guides to authenticity.
