## Literature Review

### 2.1 High-Fidelity AI Images and Screenshot-Like Visuals

The capacity to generate realistic synthetic media has expanded rapidly since the mid-2010s,
driven by advances in generative adversarial networks, diffusion models, and multimodal AI
systems (Brundage et al., 2018). Image-generation tools have also moved from specialist
software into everyday platforms, lowering the cost of producing convincing visual content.
OpenAI's GPT Image 2 model, documented with the snapshot `gpt-image-2-2026-04-21`, illustrates
this recent shift toward higher-fidelity image generation in mainstream AI systems (OpenAI,
2026a).

For this paper, the important point is not only that AI images can look like photographs. They
can also imitate text-bearing and interface-bearing visuals: posters, product cards, social
media layouts, livestream screens, chat windows, and other screenshot-like artifacts. These
formats matter because they are often consumed as contextual evidence rather than as isolated
images. A livestream screenshot, for instance, includes comments, prices, avatars, platform
icons, and timestamps that may make viewers feel they are seeing a trace of a real event.

### 2.2 Human Ability to Detect AI-Generated Images

A growing body of empirical research examines how well people can identify AI-generated
content, and the findings are consistently cautious. Nightingale and Farid (2022) demonstrated
that AI-synthesized faces were not only difficult to distinguish from real faces but were also
rated as more trustworthy. This suggests that synthetic visual content may sometimes carry an
unearned credibility advantage.

Frank et al. (2024) conducted a cross-national study with 3,002 participants across the United
States, Germany, and China. Participants were exposed to AI-generated and human-created media
across text, image, and audio modalities and asked to classify each item. The results showed
that many participants performed close to chance level, and factors such as age, education,
media literacy, and political awareness did not reliably improve detection performance.

In the domain of images specifically, Roca et al. (2025) analyzed approximately 287,000
evaluations from over 12,500 participants using data from an online detection game. Overall
accuracy was 62%, only modestly above chance. Performance varied by content type:
participants were more successful with AI-generated human portraits and less successful with
natural and urban landscapes. This finding supports the need to compare different visual
categories rather than treating all images as a single type of material.

### 2.3 Screenshots, Visual Evidence, and Media Trust

The social risk of AI-generated images is not limited to mistaken source judgement. Inwood and
Zappavigna (2024) show that screenshots play an important role in digital communication
because they are often circulated as visual evidence. They preserve a partial record of an
interface, making a post, message, or livestream appear traceable and shareable. This helps
explain why screenshot-like AI images may be persuasive even when viewers know synthetic media
exists.

Misinformation research provides further support for this concern. Guo et al. (2025) found
that realistic AI-synthesized images increased belief in false headlines when the images
appeared to provide strong evidence for the claim. DiResta and Goldstein (2024) documented
that spammers and scammers were already using AI-generated images on Facebook to attract
attention and grow audiences. These studies shift the discussion from hypothetical future
misuse to current platform-level behavior.

For media trust specifically, Metzger and Flanagin (2013) argued that online credibility
evaluation relies heavily on cognitive heuristics rather than systematic verification. Users
often depend on surface-level cues such as visual quality, interface familiarity, and perceived
professionalism. This is precisely where modern AI-generated visual content creates pressure:
the cues that make an image feel authentic are becoming easier to reproduce. Vaccari and
Chadwick (2020) further showed that synthetic media can create uncertainty that damages trust
in genuine media, a problem sometimes described as the broader erosion of epistemic confidence.

Content provenance and labeling are therefore relevant but incomplete responses. OpenAI's help
documentation explains that C2PA metadata can be attached to ChatGPT-generated images, but it
also notes that metadata may be removed when images are uploaded to many social media
platforms or edited by downstream tools (OpenAI, 2026b). This limitation matters for the
present study because ordinary users typically encounter images after they have already moved
through platform compression, reposting, cropping, or screenshotting.
